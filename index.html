Applications

All these applications are prototypes showcased for demonstration purposes. Mental health and well-being interventions, emphatically, must not be used for therapeutic purposes.

Simple Statistics is a free-to-use, no-download online resource to perform basic inferential statistical analysis.

More about Simple Statistics
This application was created as a mobile-friendly learning aid and homework check for introduction to statistics students. Currently, this version only conducts 4 t-tests, sufficient for an introduction to statistics student.

I am currently completing courses in data visualization and smartphone application development. I hope to use this knowledge in the future to develop native, lightweight smartphone application that conducts more statistical tests (non-parametrics, ANOVA, ANCOVA, blocked designs, MANCOVA, and linear, multiple, logistic regressions, etc.) and outputs graphical representaitons of the results.


Simple Breath is a minimalistic audiovisual guide for deep breathing.

More about Simple Breath
This application replicates the Box Breathing Method, a 16-second deep breathing routine where you inhale, hold, exhale, and hold for 4 seconds each. Visitors are also provided the option of relaxing ambient music. This was the first micro intervention I released and it shaped an interest in intervention research in two branching directions: 1) Multi-sensory, multi-track interventions (e.g., relaxing visuals, sounds, soothing prompts/narration), and 2) Precision psychotherapeutic techniques.

By precision I mean developing a technique with high construct validity. For example, many online "Mindfulness meditations" often overlap with self-compassion, defusion, and other discrete concepts. Although blended techniques are not inherently negative, it interests me from a research and programming perspective to precisely develop interventions of psychotherapeutic techniques.


Simple Flex is a basic visual guide for an adapted Progressive Muscle Relaxation procedure.

More about Simple Flex
The last thing anyone wants when they are relaxing is to feel negative about their body. Being mindful of positive body image and normative discontent, I chose an average adult male and female render as the body models for this webapp. You may also notice a robot as an option for the younger audience.

Visitors can personalize their intervention by selecting a realistic body image model, colours of the body and glow, and order of relaxation.


YouMatter, named in respects of #BLM, is an all-in-one, self-administered suicide risk assessment, mental health screener, and personalized treatment.

More about YouMatter
Risk Assessment (Step 1)
Visitors complete a 4-criteria suicide risk assessment for ideation, plan, means, and timeframe. These criterions are currently used by Crisis Text Line and Kids Help Phone. Those that meet all four (i.e., high-risk visitors) are immediately streamlined to the phone numbers of crisis services to get support and guided to practice a breathing exercise prompts while they connect. Low-risk visitors continue onto screening for psychological symptoms.

Screening (Step 2)
I combined the DSM–5 Self-Rated Level 1 Cross-Cutting Symptom Measure (Ages 11-17 version) with the SCOFF questionnaire to screen for potential symptoms of anxiety, depression, problematic substance use, and disordered eating. These screening tools were either designed by researchers to be self-administered by those in late childhood / adolescence and/or have been successfully validated in natural setings (i.e., insignificant differences in self-administrations for school samples vs. clinician administration).

Personalized information (Step 3)
Visitors not at imminent risk receive a personalized mental health intervention and resources. Components include: supportive words, peer vignette, calming techniques, psychoeducation, safety planning, crisis service lists, and suggested therapeutic programs. Supportive words, a portrait of a peer, and a vignette are customized based on gender and ethnicity. Calming Techniques reflect distress tolerance exercises from DBT. The psychoeducation module shows only relevant information based on potential symptoms meeting the cut-off scores from the preceeding screening section. There are also resources for therapeutic programs, crisis services, and safety planning.

Conclusion: Additional thoughts
Vignettes deserve a special mention here. What a very interesting experience writing custom vignettes based on letters and videos from the respective demographics discussing mental illness (during background research). It would be interesting to examine methodological approaches to developing effective vignettes. For example, 1) Should researchers draft a vignette based on humanistic and solution-focused principles, then have the target demographic judge whether the constructed message was helpful? What about face validity of the message? 2) Should we ask a target demographic to directly tell us what they want to hear and find helpful? 3) Should we tap into the wisdom of an older population of the target demographic to retrospectively reflect on what message (in both form and content) would have been most helpful when they were young?

Another interesting thought is the tricky balance in selecting vignette portraits. For example, "Asian" is a heterogeneous category, and recent immigrants (or displaced persons, refugees, etc.) may not find a second- or third-generation immigrant relatable based on mere looks, or content/form of the peer message. Another consideration is that some visitors would prefer to match with their "virtual peer" in terms of cultural background instead of gender and ethnicity. Finally, while there's clear value in integrating the nomothetic literature (e.g., caucasian female adolescents may benefit most from eating disorder content), finding the balance between effectively targeting common problems for a target demographic and inadvertently reinforcing a stereotype seems like something that needs further exploration (hint: nomothetic meets idiographic)!

Experimental Replications


The Emotional Stroop Task is a behavioural measure of information processing delays for emotionally salient stimuli. This version of the EST uses lexical stimuli and records reaction times (RT) by keyboard press.

Process and lessons
I recreated the EST based on Ben-Haim et al. (2016). There are two conditions (mixed vs. blocked--neutral, negative): both include a training block (25 trials) and four experimental blocks (20 trials each).

I learned a lot more than I expected such as trial size requirements, counter-balancing (which is recommended against in the Stroop in certain circumstances such as carryover effects occuring when a negative block precedes a neutral block), and inter-trial intervals.

My favorite lesson would have to be the procedure in selecting lexical stimuli (e.g., HAL frequency, orthographic neighbourhoods, valence, arousal, domain vs. idiographic selection). Although my particular recreation had a much less methodologically rigorous stimuli selection process, I am glad I am aware of these parameters for future interventions that utilize personally-relevant stimuli.

Primary study guiding the recreation:
Ben-Haim, M. S., Williams, P., Howard, Z., Mama, Y., Eidels, A., & Algom, D. (2016). The Emotional Stroop Task: Assessing Cognitive Performance under Exposure to Emotional Content. Journal of visualized experiments : JoVE, (112), 53720. https://doi.org/10.3791/53720


The Dot Probe Task is one form of attention-bias modification (ABM). This version uses lexical stimuli for training and visual stimuli (i.e., visual dot probe task) for the experimental block, and measures RT by keyboard press. The purpose of this program is to train attention towards neutral as opposed to negative stimuli (as opposed to some ABMs developing attention to positivity).

Process and lessons
I adapted an ABM based on Amir et al. (2009). There are two conditions (active vs. control): both include a training block (16 trials of lexical stimuli) and one experimental block (20 trials). The experimental blocks had trials with 80% neutral-negative and 20% neutral-neutral stimulus. The active condition replaced all neutral images with the dot probe, whereas the control replaced only 50% of the 80% neutral-negative segment of the experimental block.

This was an interesting paradigm to learn about! Although the standard for duration of fixation cross and stimuli presentation is "500ms", seemingly minute variabilities in this parameter appear to capture different processes (e.g., normal populations with greater attention than anxious populations at 100ms but not 500ms may be an indicator of ability to disengage). Despite the comedic stimuli I chose for this recreation, it was overall a great exercise to familiarize myself with counter-balancing procedures (e.g., image position, probe position).

On the implicit cognition front, I am interested in many indices (e.g., allocation, disengagement) and what recent basic research findings can improve current cognitive-bias modification paradigms. This was an exciting first step as I work towards learning more about and recreating other ABM, CBM-I, and cogntive control training programs.

Primary study guiding the recreation:
Amir, N., Beard, C., Taylor, C. T., Klumpp, H., Elias, J., Burns, M., & Chen, X. (2009). Attention training in individuals with generalized social phobia: A randomized controlled trial. Journal of consulting and clinical psychology, 77(5), 961–973. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0016685

The Face Stimuli Standardizer program is a precision time-saver that standardizes the position and size of face stimuli. The picture below illustrates what exactly that means (click picture to expand):
A visual reproduction of what the program corrects. Model photos courtesy of Shutterstock.

Researchers developing face stimuli databases run into inevitable imprecision due to photography procedures (e.g., camera position, zoom, and head position). This program aims to correct this according to parameters such as size and position, while reserving ratios and preventing distortions (i.e., no stretchy face).

This solution is possible through a facial detection algorithim. While a team could choose to "standardize" faces through a manual procedure (e.g., onion skin and resizing) in an image editing software, simple changes would require recreation of entire stimuli sets--not only laborious, but increases the margin of error. Things can get overwhelming when one considers how many stimuli is created in gender * race * [enter other social psychological parameter like attractiveness or similarity], not to mention the intra-individual factors such as emotion and emotional intensity!

Thus, this program was born from the need to reduce noise in stimuli presentation. It was programmed in Python3 and is available for public use. Instructions are included in the .py script.
